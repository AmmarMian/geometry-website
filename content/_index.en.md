Satellites such as Sentinel-1 and Sentinel-2 photograph the same places again and again. Each pixel becomes a multivariate time series — radar echoes, spectral bands, interferometric phases — recording how glaciers flow, cities subside and volcanoes deform. These measurements do not live in flat space: their natural home is curved — spheres, tori, spaces of covariance matrices.

GEOMETRY takes this curvature seriously. By computing directly on these manifolds, we aim for algorithms that learn from far fewer labelled examples, run lighter — an environmental concern as much as a practical one — and stay robust to the noise and variability inherent to remote sensing.
